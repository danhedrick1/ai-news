### Anthropic Wins Injunction Against Pentagon Blacklisting

A federal judge granted Anthropic a preliminary injunction this week, ordering the Trump administration to temporarily rescind the Defense Department's designation of Anthropic as a "supply chain risk" — the label that had effectively blacklisted the company from DoD contracts and partnerships. The ruling doesn't end the lawsuit, but it means Anthropic can operate normally in the defense space while litigation proceeds. The DoD's original designation reportedly stemmed from what court documents describe as Anthropic's "hostile manner" — which, reading between the lines, likely refers to the company's public positions on AI safety and export controls that conflicted with administration priorities.

This matters beyond Anthropic specifically. The Pentagon's supply chain risk designation authority is a blunt instrument typically aimed at foreign adversaries and compromised hardware vendors — not US-based AI labs. Using it against a domestic AI company for apparent policy disagreements sets a precedent that should alarm every AI company with views that diverge from the current administration's. The judge's willingness to grant the injunction suggests the designation was on shaky legal ground to begin with.

For builders: if you're working on government AI contracts, this case is shaping the boundaries of how the executive branch can pressure AI vendors into compliance. The fact that a *safety-focused* lab got targeted while the administration simultaneously calls for AI dominance is the kind of contradiction that will define this era. Watch the underlying lawsuit — the merits phase could produce precedent with real teeth.

### ARC-AGI-3 Drops — Agents Must Explore, Infer Goals, and Plan Without Instructions

The ARC Prize team released ARC-AGI-3, a new benchmark explicitly targeting *agentic* intelligence rather than static pattern matching. Unlike ARC-AGI-1/2, which tested fluid reasoning on visual puzzles, ARC-AGI-3 places agents in interactive, turn-based environments where they must explore unknown dynamics, infer what they're even supposed to do, and build internal world models — all without explicit task descriptions. Reddit reports at least one model scoring 36% on day one, which is simultaneously impressive and a reminder that these benchmarks get cracked fast now.

The benchmark design is a direct response to criticism that prior ARC tasks were too solvable by brute-force search. The "no explicit instructions" constraint is the hard part — it's closer to real deployment than anything we've had before.

**Source:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24621) · Also: [Reddit r/singularity](https://www.reddit.com/r/singularity/comments/1s4ouct/from_0_to_36_on_day_1_of_arcagi3/)

## crontab -l // trend watch

The dominant pattern today is **platform consolidation through data portability** — but only in one direction. Google's memory import tool is a land-grab disguised as a user-friendly feature. The message to competitors: we'll accept your users' data, but we won't be exporting ours. Apple opening Siri to third-party AI is the counter-move — commoditizing the assistant layer by turning Siri into a router. Both moves suggest the major platforms have concluded that the model layer is too commoditized to defend, so the real moat is context: your history, your preferences, your habits. Builders should assume that every major platform will have "import from anywhere" within 12 months, and the lock-in won't be the model — it'll be accumulated context that's never quite portable enough.

The second thread worth tracking is **defense tech's acceleration**. Shield AI at $2.8B, the Navy's $900M automation investment, Canada's Juno, and the Anthropic/Pentagon legal battle all point to the same thing: the military-AI interface is getting formalized fast, on both the procurement and the adversarial sides. The Anthropic case is particularly notable — a *safety-focused* lab got blacklisted, suggesting the DoD wants AI vendors who don't push back on deployment decisions. That's a values selection pressure that will shape which AI companies can participate in this market.

Finally, **the benchmark arms race is real but don't get too attached**: ARC-AGI-3 launched today and hit 36% on day one. The community's ability to crack new benchmarks within days of release is outpacing our ability to design benchmarks that hold up. This is actually fine — it means capabilities are advancing — but it means any single benchmark score you're using to evaluate a model is probably stale within a quarter. Build your own evals for your actual task distribution. That's the only benchmark that matters.

**Hot take**: SoftBank borrowing $40B to double down on OpenAI, while Microsoft posts its worst quarter since 2008 partly *because of* AI costs, is the clearest signal yet that we're in a phase where the infrastructure bet is massively ahead of the revenue realization. Someone is going to be very right or very wrong about the timeline here, and the bridge loan structure suggests SoftBank knows it.
